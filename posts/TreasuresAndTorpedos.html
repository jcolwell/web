Treasures and Torpedoes

Programming and Development in Unity3D

Posted on Sept 4, 2015

In my third quarter at The Art Institute of Vancouver I had the opportunity to learn about programming and game development for Unity3D. During the 11 week course my classmates and I got the chance to see how Unity works and how to create a platformer and a shooter.

In the last four weeks of the course we had a chance to put this newly gathered knowledge to the test. We formed into groups and got to work to making a game. I had the pleasure of working with Cole Rudniski, a programmer, and two game designers; Kevin Hill and Victoria Wojcik. And with that, Torpedo’s Treasures was born!

…Ok well maybe not quite like that. First we needed to plan what we want to do; a submarine game that the player could maneuver a 3D environment with the aim of collecting Davy Jones’ treasure. Ok that sounds like a start but is there anything else we can add? Enemy submarines to shoot down with torpedoes? Ah that sounds like an excellent idea! But wait, how does the player collect Davy Jones' treasure? How about a magnet? This is an idea to work with!

 

The Player

Now that we have an idea of what we want to create it was time to get to work. I had the pleasure of creating the scripts to allow the player to maneuver their submarine and collect the treasure.

Implementing the controls for our player was very straight forward. Victoria and I had discussed what our controls should be. Making it just an issue of coding it up. Cole simplified everything even further by turning off gravity to our game world allowing us to mimic buoyancy.

The traditional FPS controls of “WASD” made the foundation of our controls. “W” and “S” allowing the player to move forward and back respectively, while “A” and “D” would allow the player to roll their vessel left and right. The mouse provide the pitch and yaw authority to complete the player movement.

Since the underlying mechanics of motion for the player are RigidBody.MovePosition() commands. However, this did introduce a small bug. When the player crashed into another object or took an impact from an enemy torpedo, the vessel would accumulate rotations and movement that the player could not cancel out. To fix this we opted to set all rotations and movements to zero before applying the player’s inputs, allowing the player to maintain control.

 
The Magnet

When I was discussing the design of the magnet with Kevin and Victoria, we agreed that making it easy to use from a gameplay perspective was important to us. To that end, we decided the magnet would have a fixed extension range and automatically retract once it either fully extended, or made contact with our treasure.

The movement of the magnet was powered by a Vector3.Lerp() call in the MagnetController’s Update(). I used two functions to drive the direction of the lerp: RetractClaw()simplified the process of retracting when making connection with treasure, while ExtendClaw() made extending the magnet on the player’s direction easy to connect with the PlayerController.

To physically move treasure once the magnet made contract we took advantage of Unity’s parental hierarchy and made the treasure a child object of the magnet. Doing this allowed the positional updates that were applied to the magnet to also affect the treasure. To destroy and reward players for collecting treasures we used trigger volumes attached to the player. When the magnet retracted enough, it crossed into the trigger volume, destroying the treasure and rewarding the player.

Lastly, the magnet also made use of Unity’s LineRenderer. We used this feature to draw a line between the player’s submarine and its magnet; give the illusion that the magnet was connect to the player’s submarine.
